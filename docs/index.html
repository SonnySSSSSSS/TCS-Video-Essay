Trịnh Công Sơn — Painting Book Introduction

Flowers decayed.. turned to mushrooms along scarred roads, as mothers chased overfilled carts, clapping.. pleading for their sons.

Beside that memory—silhouettes, boy and girl.. sitting close on a moonlit summer wharf.. Both wanting, needing.. only to be next to the other.

These two rêves contraires sighed together.. haunting the mind of a man. As contradiction or something needing resolution? No.. But as what life simply is.. and must be.

His name was Trịnh Công Sơn. Vietnamese musician, painter, poet. 1939–2001. And he gave poetic voice to -- yet another-- Vietnamese generation besieged by conflict; But he sang for both sides, North and South, and still today both sides sing his songs — at funerals, in cafes, or alone in a room with too much space to feel. He wrote more than six hundred songs. Rarely told you how to feel about the thing he was looking at. But looking at it together, you just know that you feel the same. He made lullabies for cannons firing in the night. Not just for his time, but the thousands of years of history they fired throughout the motherland. He also gave a woman's thin fingers dignity through a thousand years of holding them in his hand. His songs are named the way colors or a dream is named: Mưa Hồng [Pink Rain], Nắng Thủy Tinh [Glass Sunlight], Hạ Trắng [White Summer].

But everything was held in paradox. In tension.

The title Đại bác ru đêm is the thing, itself. It's not an argument to be made, it's really a feeling of seeing it and knowing what the history and context of what it means. Tenderness next to violence inside a cycle that returns over and over again in Vietnam. Millions of Vietnamese lives displaced, lost, and searching for meaning under duress. This friction, this karma, is the feeling of being in it. His compositions handed it to you.

That is the legacy of his music written. But the legacy of his art.. only now is beginning to be written. But if we mistake his music for his painting, we will see shade on the wrong side of his face. Whereas, perhaps , if we move just a bit to the side, we might see something he too realized.

Late in his life he said this:

I searched for myself in music and poetry. I still hadn't found enough of my own face.

Six hundred songs. All of Vietnam reflecting his words back to him. And the man who wrote them still said that he couldn't find his own face.

Because a mirror .. doesn't work in a dream.

Some things one medium can hold and another cannot. A song travels forward in time. Controlled is the order of your attention — You've no choice but to hear that order in which the composer intended. Shackled by time.. by sequence, audience and performer moving through it together -- that is a song.

A painting is not a song.

A painting sits still.. and sequence belongs solely to the viewer. Your eye enters where it wants to.. a shoulder? A sky? The empty space at the edge? And forgotten is that everything you are not looking at still was there, from the edges of your awareness, shaping that which you feel of what you see.

For a man whose central subject was the ungraspable this. Was. Necessary.

He didn't stop composing. Two expressions flowed from the same essence. But painting.. painting made a room no other could comprehend —present.. not named, he could show.. things that could not be sung.

There is a painting — a figure, barely human, a silhouette that might be a woman or might be something dissolving into one. A wine cup below. A vagina rendered without modesty or apology or explanation. Desire, loneliness, dissolution, appetite — all present, none named. You cannot look at it and say what it means. You cannot look away and say it meant nothing. This could never have been a lyric. It could never have been a title. To put it into language would be to choose one of those meanings and kill the others. The painting holds them all at once, alive, without resolving. [maybe]

A green landscape in the background and a woman in green in the foreground.

Notice the compression: In the sentence, the adjective.. then the noun.. that sequence unfolds, you understand meaning as it unfolds, putting the scene together in sequence. The meaning comes together over time.

But the painting arrives. That time you would've spent waiting for it to unfold is absent. The meaning doesn't arrive. The meaning is waiting for you to sense it. This is what it means to hold his art.

This way of seeing doesn't require a canvas.

Your father. The way he sits with one foot always slightly forward, always slightly ready to rise if someone needs something. Your mother's arms, always slightly open. Balanced against the weight of a critical word too quick to dismiss you, or an aloofness that didn't make you feel cared for.

You've read those lines. Now close your eyes and see the actual scene. Not the words — the person. Notice how different that is. The sentence gave you information. The seeing gives you them. Paradox and all.

Now, one last thing. I'm going to describe a painting to you, but I'm not going to show it to you. Not yet.

In 2000, the year before he died, TCS painted his last self-portrait. The body is only half of a shape.. dissolved. The neck bends in a way necks don't bend. Skin .. soil like .. not flesh any longer. And the glasses and eyes feel too tired to hold a form recognizable. If you didn't know this was a self portrait by TCS, you would not know it was one by TCS. No form. No soul. Just. Illness. But.. He added a hat. After all of that. A green fishing hat.

Hold that Trinh Cong Son paradox. Find the portrait in this book. Then.. experience the arriving.

Seeing isn't a philosophy. It's a direction. Now let's turn the page; let's look together.

Notes

French: contrary dreams, or opposing reveries. The phrase is not a standard literary term — it is used here to name a felt condition: two incompatible visions that coexist without cancelling each other. War and intimacy, loss and desire, held in the same mind at the same time.

Trịnh Công Sơn's anti-war songs were embraced in the South during the conflict, and after reunification in 1975, his music was adopted in the North as well. He is one of very few cultural figures whose work transcended the political division. Both sides claimed him; neither side fully contained him.

Literally "Cannons Lullaby the Night" or "Cannons Sing the Night to Sleep." One of Trịnh Công Sơn's most well-known anti-war compositions. The title itself performs the paradox it describes: the instrument of destruction recast as the instrument of comfort. The cannon becomes the mother; the bombardment becomes the lullaby.

Mưa Hồng (Pink Rain), Nắng Thủy Tinh (Glass Sunlight), Hạ Trắng (White Summer) — these titles name sensory impossibilities. Rain is not pink. Sunlight is not glass. Summer is not white. But each title creates an image that is immediately, emotionally legible. They function like painting titles: not descriptions, but invitations to feel.

This quote is attributed to Trịnh Công Sơn in his later years, as recounted by friends and family. The full sentiment — that six hundred songs were not enough to find his own face — speaks to his turn toward painting as a medium that could hold what language and melody could not.

The distinction drawn here — between a song's temporal sequence and a painting's spatial simultaneity — echoes Lessing's Laocoön (1766), which distinguished between arts of time and arts of space. But the essay's point is not theoretical. It is that Trịnh Công Sơn needed both, and that certain things he carried could only be held in the medium that does not move forward.

Trịnh Công Sơn died on April 1, 2001, of complications from diabetes and liver disease. The self-portrait described — painted in his final year — is among his last known works. The green hat, added to a body already dissolving, is the paradox made visible: adornment in the face of disintegration, a small act of life insisted upon at the edge of death.

Estimates of Trịnh Công Sơn's total output vary. The commonly cited figure is over 600 compositions, though some sources place it higher. Many were written in single sittings, often at social gatherings. A significant number remain unrecorded or exist only in manuscript form held by the Trịnh family.
