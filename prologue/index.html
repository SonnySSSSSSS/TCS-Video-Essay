In Buddhism, there is the concept of Śūnyatā –
emptiness, the void from which all things arise.
And from that emptiness, awareness emerged.
To be. To see.
What is it that I see?
From this seeing came the Logos –
the word, the pattern, the order.
Stars scattered across the heavens.
Planets took their orbits.
Cosmic formations bloomed in the darkness.
Through countless kalpas –
eons beyond measure –
this unfolding continued.
Until on one small blue planet,
in a spiral arm of the Milky Way galaxy...
In the ancient city of Huế,
by the Perfume River...
A man was born who saw life differently.
He saw strawberries.
Not just the fruit, but what the fruit means.
Sweetness. The precious taste of a moment.
Everything he loved became strawberries in his vision.
But why strawberries?
Why this particular way of seeing?
There is an old story in Zen Buddhism:
A man traveling across a field encountered a tiger.
He fled, the tiger chasing after him.
Coming to a precipice, he caught hold of a wild vine
and swung himself down over the edge.
The tiger sniffed at him from above.
Trembling, the man looked down to where,
far below, another tiger waited.

Two mice – one white, one black –
began to gnaw away at the vine.
The man saw a luscious strawberry near him.
Grasping the vine with one hand,
he plucked the strawberry with the other.
How sweet it tasted.
That is how he saw life.
He saw the tigers.
He knew the vine was fraying.
He tasted the strawberry anyway.
And from that knowing –
from seeing both the sweetness and the abyss –
his art was born.
From his grief –
rivers.
